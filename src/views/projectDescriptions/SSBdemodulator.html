SSB Demodulator

Building a single sideband demodulator with a team as part of a hardware design course, meant to integrate together to form a fully functioning radio transmitting/receiving in the 8-16 MHz (HF) band.

Project Overview

A single sideband (SSB) demodulator designed to take in high frequency (HF) radio signals, and demodulate them down to audio frequencies for playback. This project was part of a larger team effort to build a complete HF radio system, with other team members responsible for the transmitter, power amplifier, and antenna systems.

This project focuses on demodulating single-sideband (SSB) HF radio signals using an entirely analog signal chain. Signals in the 8‚Äì16 MHz range are first buffered and split into two paths, where op-amp phase shifters generate a precise 90¬∞ quadrature relationship. These I and Q signals are then fed into balanced mixers driven by a synchronized local oscillator, performing quadrature downconversion directly to baseband. By mathematically recombining the I/Q outputs, one sideband is constructively reinforced while the other is suppressed.

The resulting low-frequency audio signal is then filtered and amplified for output audio.

Challenges

One of the main challenges was just understanding how the phase shifting and quadrature mixing worked in practice, as well as ensuring that the analog components were precise enough to maintain signal integrity. We actually ended up running into an issue where our phase shifters would fail to suppress a particular spike at the 1 MHz range, which when amplified caused some static in the audio output.

Another challenge was that during the Altium design phase, we had accidentally swapped the + and - inputs of the low pass filter after the phase shifting stage, which basically meant zero signal output after shifting. Because this was the only component we chose that was not SMD, we were lucky enough to be able to bend the pins and jumper the connections to fix the issue without having to remake the entire PCB.

Lastly, we also had accidentally designed the pinout of the board to be mirrored against what the main motherboard we were meant to plug into. This meant for us to integrate with the rest of the subsystems, we had to create a custom jumper adapter to flip the connections around during testing.
